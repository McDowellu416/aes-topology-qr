What is a BUS Topology?

A bus topology connects devices to one main communication line, often called the bus or backbone.

Data travels along this shared path, and devices receive the traffic that passes along the line. Older Ethernet networks commonly used this type of layout.

Benefits of a BUS Topology

  • Simple layout and easy to understand.
  • Can use less cable than some other topologies.
  • Can be suitable for small or temporary networks.
  • Devices connect into one shared communication path.

Limitations of a BUS Topology

  • If the main backbone fails, the network can be badly affected.
  • Performance can reduce as more devices are added.
  • Fault finding can be more difficult than with a star topology.
  • Older bus networks could suffer from collisions on the shared medium.

RAF/AES Example

A bus topology is useful for understanding older network designs and the idea of a shared transmission path.

For Communication Infrastructure Technicians, it helps explain why modern networks normally favour switched star layouts, where individual cable runs and central patching make fault finding and management easier.
