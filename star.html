What is a STAR Topology?

A star topology connects each device back to a central device, usually a switch.

Each device has its own connection to the centre. If one end device or cable fails, the rest of the network can usually continue working. However, if the central switch fails, the connected devices may lose network access.

Benefits of a STAR Topology

  • Easy to add or remove devices.
  • A fault with one end device usually does not stop the rest of the network.
  • Fault finding can be easier because devices have individual links to the centre.
  • Common in modern LANs using switches.

Limitations of a STAR Topology

  • The central switch is a critical point of failure.
  • Requires more cabling than some older/simple layouts.
  • Performance depends on the capacity and configuration of the central device.
  • Poor patching or poor cable management can make the network harder to maintain.

RAF/AES Example

In a training building, classrooms, offices, printers and other devices may all connect back to switches located in a cabinet or network room.

For Communication Infrastructure Technicians, this is important because correct cabling, patching, labelling and switch connectivity help keep the network reliable and maintainable.
